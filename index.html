01 · The Decoupling

Capital markets and the real economy have parted ways.

Through the exact window in which Southeast Asia’s venture deal count sank to its lowest level in nearly a decade, SME hiring activity on Glints more than tripled. Small employers do not hire because sentiment improved. They hire because orders came in.

02 · The New Employer Class

Half of the SMEs hiring today have never hired on a platform before.

In H1 2026, 49% of small employers posting on Glints were doing so for the first time ever — businesses entering the formal hiring market, many making their first hire, full stop. And the fastest-growing employer class is the smallest: companies with fewer than ten people are now nearly one in three hiring SMEs, up from one in five two years ago.

03 · What SMEs Hire Now

The boom is the real economy — plus a creator class that barely existed three years ago.

The fastest-rising SME roles are not what the AI-doom discourse predicts. They are drivers, bakers, production workers, sales promoters — the jobs of physical commerce growing again. Alongside them, a creator-economy hiring cluster grew 53% year on year: live-commerce hosts, social media managers, e-commerce operators. Content Creator is now a top-8 SME role outright.

04 · Speed Is the Product

An SME job posted at lunch has applicants before the coffee is cold.

SME hiring runs on a different clock from enterprise recruitment. No requisition cycles, no two-week job-ad campaigns. The median SME job on Glints receives its first application in about 12 minutes, and 94.6% receive one within 24 hours. On the employer side, half of all shortlisting decisions happen within a day of the application arriving.

06 · Methodology & What We Left Out

Built to be checked.

An index from one platform’s data is only as good as its definitions — so here they are, along with what we deliberately excluded.

Definitions

  • SME — employers with ≤200 staff (self-reported at registration)
  • Micro-employer — fewer than 10 staff
  • Hiring activity — job postings created in period, spam/removed postings excluded
  • First-time employer — first-ever posting by that business on the platform
  • Index — monthly postings, January 2024 = 100

Coverage

Six markets: Indonesia, Vietnam, Singapore, Malaysia, the Philippines and Taiwan, Jan 2024 – Jun 2026. Indonesia — home to roughly two-thirds of ASEAN’s MSMEs — contributes the large majority of index volume; country mix is published alongside the data so no aggregate hides it.

What we deliberately left out

  • Anything about Glints’ own commercial performance — this is a report about the segment, not about us
  • Shortlisting-speed data before 2025 (instrumentation for that signal launched Jan 2025)
  • Role categories whose growth reflects taxonomy relabelling rather than real hiring

Honest limits

One platform’s lens, however wide, is still one lens. Company size is self-reported. And whether the current acceleration is structural or a post-downturn catch-up is a question the next editions of this index exist to answer.

A Letter from Glints

The Other Southeast Asia

There are two Southeast Asias right now. The first one lives on LinkedIn and in the tech press: funding charts pointing down, layoff trackers filling up, every second post about AI coming for white-collar jobs. Spend a day there and the rational conclusion is that the region’s best years are behind it.

We’ve been building in the second one since 2013. It looks nothing like the first.

Last month we met a woman who runs three coffee shops in Jakarta. She needed a barista by Saturday, because outlet number three was opening whether she was staffed or not. Her hiring stack was Instagram stories, a WhatsApp group, and hope. The funding winter never reached her, because no funding ever did. She hired because a third outlet opened.

And she is the market. The overwhelming majority of Southeast Asia’s enterprises are small businesses, and they employ most of its workforce. Almost none of the hiring infrastructure built in the last twenty years was built for them.

What the data says

This half we started counting properly. Three things in the first edition of this index surprised even us.

First, the decoupling. SME job postings have more than tripled since January 2024 — through the exact window in which the region’s venture deal count fell to its lowest level in nearly a decade. Capital markets and the real economy have simply parted ways.

Second, half of the small employers hiring today have never hired on a platform before. Tens of thousands of businesses each half-year are entering the formal hiring market for the first time — and the fastest-growing employer class is the smallest, companies of fewer than ten people.

Third, what they’re hiring surprised us most. The fastest-rising roles are the real economy’s: drivers, bakers, production workers, field sales. And next to them, a creator class that barely existed three years ago — live-commerce hosts, content creators, social media operators — growing fifty percent a year and paying wages that compete with accounting.

Why the platforms couldn’t see this

Job platforms grew up serving enterprises, because that’s where the budgets were. Annual contracts, ad packages priced in the thousands, employer branding suites. All rational, for a bank hiring five hundred people a year.

A three-outlet coffee chain needs five qualified candidates in a chat thread by tomorrow, at a price near a single day of one store’s revenue. Ask her to post an ad and wait two weeks and you’ve already lost her. Serving this segment takes different physics — pricing sized to a single hire, chat-first workflows, matching that does the shortlisting an owner has no time to do. We spent a decade learning those physics, mostly the hard way, and we got a good number of the decisions wrong the first time. That decade is why this data exists at all.

The AI part everyone has backwards

The prevailing story is that AI destroys jobs, and inside large companies some of that is clearly happening. At the small end we’re watching a different mechanic. AI collapses the minimum viable size of a company. The five-person business that would once have died survives, grows, and hires person six, seven and eight — often for roles nobody had a name for two years ago. It also collapses the cost of serving that business: screening, shortlisting and salary benchmarking that once needed an expensive human now happen in seconds inside a chat thread. The prize for whoever serves this segment well is bringing millions of businesses into the formal hiring market for the first time.

Where we could be wrong

The funding winter is real. AI disruption of white-collar work is real, and we think it gets worse before it gets better inside large organisations. An index built from one platform’s data has limits too, which is why we’ve published the methodology and what we deliberately left out. We’re optimistic about a segment we can measure; whether everything else recovers is a different question, and probably not ours to answer. And whether this acceleration is structural or a lag is exactly what the next editions of this index exist to find out.

But if you only price Southeast Asia off its venture funding charts, you’re pricing the wrong asset. The builders never got the memo that they were supposed to stop.

The barista, by the way, started on Monday.
